# Climate Change Lecture - Academic Listening

**Level:** B2

**Duration:** 10 minutes
**Topic:** Climate change causes and effects
**Lecturer:** Dr. Sarah Williams, Environmental Science

## Lecture Transcript

Good morning everyone. Today we're going to explore one of the most pressing issues facing our planet: climate change. I'll be discussing the causes, the current effects we're observing, and briefly touch on potential solutions.

Let's start with the basics. Climate change refers to long-term shifts in global temperatures and weather patterns. While climate has varied throughout Earth's history, the current warming trend is particularly significant because it's happening much faster than historical changes, and overwhelming scientific evidence shows it's primarily caused by human activities.


The main driver is greenhouse gas emissions, particularly carbon dioxide, or CO2. When we burn fossil fuels like coal, oil, and natural gas for energy, transportation, and industry, we release massive amounts of CO2 into the atmosphere. This gas traps heat from the sun, creating what we call the greenhouse effect.

To put this in perspective, atmospheric CO2 levels have increased by about 50% since the Industrial Revolution began in the mid-1700s. Pre-industrial CO2 concentration was approximately 280 parts per million. Today, it's over 420 parts per million and climbing.

Deforestation is another major contributor. Trees absorb CO2, so when we cut down forests for agriculture or urban development, we not only lose that carbon absorption capacity, but the decomposing trees also release stored carbon back into the atmosphere.

Agriculture, particularly livestock farming, produces methane, another potent greenhouse gas. Methane traps heat much more effectively than CO2, though it doesn't persist in the atmosphere as long.


Now, what effects are we actually seeing? The most obvious is rising global temperatures. The past decade has been the warmest on record, with 2023 showing temperatures approximately 1.2 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels. That might not sound like much, but even small temperature increases have significant consequences.

Ice melting is accelerating dramatically. The Arctic sea ice extent has declined by about 13% per decade since 1979. Greenland's ice sheet is losing mass at an average rate of 280 billion tons per year. Antarctic ice loss has tripled in the last decade.

This melting contributes to rising sea levels, which have increased by about 20 centimeters since 1880, with the rate of increase accelerating. Coastal communities worldwide face flooding risks, and some low-lying island nations like the Maldives and Tuvalu face existential threats.

We're also seeing more frequent and intense extreme weather events. Hurricanes are becoming more powerful. Heat waves are longer and more severe. Droughts in some regions are causing water shortages and agricultural failures, while other areas experience increased flooding.

Ecosystems are under stress. Coral reefs, often called the rainforests of the sea, are experiencing widespread bleaching due to warmer ocean temperatures. Species are migrating to cooler areas or, if unable to adapt quickly enough, facing extinction. The current rate of species loss is estimated to be 100 to 1,000 times higher than the natural background rate.


These environmental changes have direct human consequences. Food security is threatened as changing weather patterns affect crop yields. Water scarcity affects billions of people. Climate-related disasters are displacing populations, creating what we call climate refugees.

Health impacts include increased heat-related illnesses, expanded ranges for disease-carrying insects like mosquitoes, and worsened air quality affecting respiratory health.


So what can be done? The solutions fall into two categories: mitigation and adaptation.

Mitigation means reducing greenhouse gas emissions. This requires transitioning from fossil fuels to renewable energy sources like solar and wind power, improving energy efficiency, protecting and restoring forests, and changing agricultural practices.

The Paris Agreement, signed by nearly every country, aims to limit global warming to well below 2 degrees Celsius, ideally 1.5 degrees, compared to pre-industrial levels. Achieving this requires cutting global emissions by about 45% by 2030 and reaching net-zero by 2050.

Adaptation means preparing for changes we can't prevent. This includes building flood defenses, developing drought-resistant crops, and planning climate-resilient infrastructure.


In conclusion, climate change is not a distant future problem - it's happening now. The science is clear, the effects are observable, and the solutions, while challenging, are available. What we do in this decade will largely determine the severity of climate impacts for generations to come.

Next lecture, we'll dive deeper into renewable energy technologies and examine case studies of countries successfully transitioning away from fossil fuels. Please read Chapter 7 in your textbook before then.
