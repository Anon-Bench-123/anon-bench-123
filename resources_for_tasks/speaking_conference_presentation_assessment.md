## Presentation Excerpt (Key sections - full presentation was 20 minutes)

### Opening (Minutes 1-2)

Good afternoon, distinguished colleagues. Thank you for the opportunity to present my research today. My presentation will focus on the relationship between urban green spaces and mental health outcomes in North African cities, with particular emphasis on data collected from Algiers, Tunis, and Casablanca.

As you know, urbanization in North Africa has accelerated dramatically over the past three decades. This rapid urban growth has often occurred at the expense of green spaces, raising important questions about the impact on public health. My research attempts to quantify this relationship through both quantitative survey data and qualitative interviews.

### Middle Section (Minutes 8-10)

...Now, turning to our methodology. We employed a mixed-methods approach combining surveys of 3,000 residents across the three cities with in-depth interviews with 150 participants. The survey instrument measured several variables including self-reported mental wellbeing, frequency of green space use, proximity to nearest green space, and various demographic factors.

Our findings reveal several interesting patterns. First, there is a statistically significant correlation between regular access to green spaces and reported mental wellbeing, even after controlling for socioeconomic status, age, and education level. Specifically, residents who live within 500 meters of a park or green space report 23% higher satisfaction with their mental health compared to those living further away.

However, and this is crucial, the mere presence of green space is not sufficient. Our qualitative data reveals that the quality and perceived safety of these spaces significantly moderates the relationship...

### Question Handling Section (After presentation)

**Q1**: Thank you for that comprehensive presentation. I'm curious about how you controlled for selection bias - couldn't it be that people who are already more mentally healthy choose to live near parks?

**A**: That's an excellent question, and it's something we grappled with extensively during the research design. We addressed this in two ways. First, in our survey, we included questions about residential history - how long people had lived in their current location and whether proximity to green space was a factor in their housing choice. Second, we conducted a longitudinal analysis with a subset of 500 participants who we surveyed again after 18 months. This allowed us to track changes in mental wellbeing following residential moves. While we cannot entirely eliminate the selection bias concern, these approaches help us to strengthen our causal claims.

**Q2**: You mentioned perceived safety as an important moderating factor. Could you elaborate on that?

**A**: Certainly. In our qualitative interviews, many participants noted that while they lived near parks, they didn't feel comfortable using them due to safety concerns - particularly women and elderly residents. This was especially pronounced in parks with poor lighting or inadequate maintenance. So we found that physical proximity alone doesn't translate to mental health benefits unless the space is both accessible and perceived as safe. This has important policy implications for urban planning.
