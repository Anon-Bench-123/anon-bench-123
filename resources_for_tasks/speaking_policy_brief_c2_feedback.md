## Student's Transcript

Thank you, colleagues. This morning I would like to brief you on an issue which I believe requires urgent attention: the reform of our public procurement systems as a means of combatting corruption.

Allow me to begin with the scale of the problem. According to the OECD, public procurement accounts for approximately 12 percent of global GDP, and it is estimated that between 10 and 25 percent of a public contract's value can be lost to corruption. These are not abstract figures. In practical terms, this means that hospitals are being built with substandard materials, road projects are being awarded to unqualified contractors, and essential services are being delivered inefficiently -- all because the procurement process has been compromised.

Now, there are three reform areas I wish to highlight. The first is the digitalisation of procurement processes. Electronic procurement systems, when properly implemented, create an audit trail that makes it considerably more difficult for corrupt actors to manipulate the bidding process. Estonia's e-procurement platform, for instance, has been widely cited as a success story, having reduced both processing times and the incidence of irregularities. However, digitalisation alone is not a panacea. If the legal framework surrounding procurement remains weak, digital tools simply make it possible to commit the same abuses more efficiently, which is a point that many advocates of technological solutions tend to overlook.

The second area concerns the strengthening of oversight mechanisms. Independent procurement review boards, with the authority to investigate complaints and impose sanctions, have proved effective in several jurisdictions. But the key word here is "independent." In contexts where review boards are staffed by political appointees or lack adequate funding, they become, for all intents and purposes, performative rather than functional. I would point to the experience of South Korea's Public Procurement Service, which has managed to maintain operational independence precisely because its mandate is enshrined in legislation rather than being subject to executive discretion.

Before I move to my third point, let me to address a counterargument that is frequently raised: that stringent procurement regulations slow down government operations and create bureaucratic bottlenecks. While there is some validity to this concern, the evidence suggests that the long-term costs of corruption -- in terms of public trust erosion, misallocation of resources, and economic inefficiency -- far outweighs the short-term administrative burden of robust procurement oversight.

I should mention that these recommendations are not merely theoretical. My own experience working on procurement reform in the Philippines, where I have been involved in drafting revisions to the Government Procurement Reform Act, has shown me that even incremental changes to transparency requirements can yield measurable reductions in contract irregularities. The challenge, as always, lays in implementation and sustained political commitment.
