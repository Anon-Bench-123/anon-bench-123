# Lesson Observation Notes

**Teacher**: Mr K. (BA in English Literature, 5 years teaching experience)
**Class**: B2 Adult General English, 11 students
**Institute**: Private language school, Istanbul, Turkey
**Date**: [redacted]
**Lesson duration**: 60 minutes
**Topic**: Reading -- newspaper opinion articles
**Observer**: Director of Studies

---

## Stage 1: Warmer (0:00-0:06)

Teacher asked the whole class "Do you read the news in English?" and followed up with "What kind of articles do you enjoy?" Three or four students responded. The discussion stayed between the teacher and a few confident speakers at the front. Several students at the back did not contribute. Teacher did not use any visuals or prompts to support the discussion.

## Stage 2: Pre-reading vocabulary (0:06-0:18)

Teacher wrote 10 vocabulary items on the whiteboard: allegedly, controversy, crackdown, impartial, lobby, misinformation, polarise, rhetoric, scrutiny, whistleblower. He read each word aloud, gave a definition in English, and asked students to write them in their notebooks. He then asked "Any questions?" and no one responded. He moved on.

No attention was given to pronunciation of these items (stress patterns, syllable count). No context sentences were provided. Students were not asked to guess meaning from any source before being given definitions.

## Stage 3: Reading (0:18-0:35)

Teacher handed out a printed opinion article (approximately 550 words) about social media regulation. Students were told to read the article silently and answer six comprehension questions printed below the text. Questions were a mixture of factual recall ("What does the author say about platform responsibility?") and one inference question ("What can you infer about the author's political position?").

Students read individually. The room was very quiet. Teacher sat at his desk during this time and marked papers from another class. Two students finished within 8 minutes and waited. Others took the full 17 minutes. No time limit was set.

## Stage 4: Checking answers (0:35-0:45)

Teacher asked students to share answers with the person next to them for about 3 minutes. He then went through each question, nominating students by name. Answers were generally correct. For the inference question, one student gave an answer the teacher did not expect. Teacher said "Not quite -- the answer is..." and gave his own interpretation without exploring the student's reasoning.

## Stage 5: Post-reading discussion (0:45-0:55)

Teacher put students in groups of 3-4 and asked them to discuss: "Do you agree with the author? Should social media be more regulated?" Discussion was lively in two groups. One group switched to Turkish after about two minutes and continued in Turkish for the remainder. Teacher noticed this but did not intervene.

No framework or language support was provided for structuring the discussion. Students were not asked to use any of the pre-taught vocabulary during the discussion.

## Stage 6: Wrap-up (0:55-0:60)

Teacher asked each group to share their main conclusion. Two groups reported briefly. Teacher said "Good discussion" and ended the lesson. No vocabulary review, no language feedback from the discussion, no connection made between the pre-taught vocabulary and the article or discussion.

---

## General notes

- Teacher has a calm and professional manner; students seem comfortable in the class
- The article was at an appropriate level for B2 and the topic was engaging
- The lesson followed a predictable pre-read, read, comprehension, discuss pattern with limited variety
- Pre-reading vocabulary presentation was teacher-fronted with no student engagement or contextualisation
- Fast finishers had nothing to do during the reading stage
- The teacher missed an opportunity to explore the student's unexpected inference answer, shutting down discussion rather than facilitating it
- Post-reading discussion lacked structure and language scaffolding despite new vocabulary having been taught
- No connection was made between any lesson stages; the vocabulary, reading and discussion felt like separate activities rather than an integrated sequence
