# Lesson Observation Notes

**Teacher**: Mr A. (MA in Applied Linguistics, 18 years teaching experience, CELTA trainer)
**Class**: B1 Lower-Secondary English, 26 students (ages 13-14)
**Institute**: Private bilingual school, Rabat, Morocco
**Date**: [redacted]
**Lesson duration**: 50 minutes
**Topic**: Listening -- understanding directions and locations
**Observer**: Head of English Department

---

## Stage 1: Lead-in (0:00-0:07)

Teacher projected a simple street map and asked students in pairs to discuss: "If a tourist asked you for directions to the train station in Rabat, what would you say?" Pairs talked for about 3 minutes. Teacher then elicited responses from four pairs, writing useful phrases on the board ("Go straight", "Turn left/right", "It's next to", "It's opposite"). Students were engaged and several pairs produced good attempts.

## Stage 2: Pre-listening vocabulary (0:07-0:12)

Teacher quickly reviewed 6 key vocabulary items needed for the listening: roundabout, junction, pedestrian crossing, block, landmark, opposite. He used the projected map to point to locations that illustrated each word. Students matched words to locations on their own copy of the map in pairs. Efficient and clear.

## Stage 3: First listening -- gist (0:12-0:20)

Teacher played an audio recording (approximately 2 minutes) of a tourist asking three different people for directions to a museum. Sound quality was adequate -- played through a portable speaker. Students listened for the gist: "Does the tourist find the museum?" After listening, students discussed in pairs. Teacher took a quick show-of-hands vote. Most students answered correctly.

## Stage 4: Second listening -- detail (0:20-0:32)

Teacher handed out a worksheet with three tasks:
1. Number the directions in the correct order (sequence task)
2. Mark the tourist's route on a blank map
3. Identify which person gave the clearest directions and why

Students listened again. Teacher played the recording twice more during this stage. Students worked individually on the tasks, then compared in pairs. Teacher circulated actively, checking maps and helping with the sequencing task.

When going through the answers, teacher used the projector to trace the route on the map. He was thorough with tasks 1 and 2. For task 3, he asked three students for their opinions. All three chose the same person. Teacher confirmed the answer and moved on without exploring why the other two sets of directions were less clear.

## Stage 5: Post-listening activity (0:32-0:44)

Teacher put students into groups of 3. One student was the "tourist" and two were "local residents." Using a different projected map, the tourist had to ask for directions to three places. The residents had to give directions. Groups rotated roles after each round.

The activity generated good energy. However, teacher stayed at one side of the room helping one particular group for about 5 minutes and did not circulate evenly. Some groups on the far side finished early and began talking about other things. Two groups were observed giving directions using only "go" and pointing gestures, without attempting the target language from the lesson.

## Stage 6: Wrap-up (0:44-0:50)

Teacher asked students to return to seats. He said "OK, what phrases did we learn today for giving directions?" and elicited several from the class. He wrote them on the board again. He then set homework: "Draw a map from your house to the school and write the directions in English."

No feedback on language use from the post-listening activity was given. No errors from the speaking practice were addressed. The homework was set but no example or model was provided.

---

## General notes

- Teacher demonstrated strong command of the lesson structure and materials
- Lead-in and pre-listening stages were efficient and well-contextualised
- Listening tasks were well-graded and progressed from gist to detail appropriately
- The audio was played a sensible number of times
- Task 3 (evaluating which directions were clearest) had potential for developing critical thinking but was not fully exploited
- Post-listening speaking activity was well-designed but monitoring was uneven; some groups received significant support while others received none
- Despite strong language awareness, teacher did not provide any feedback on errors from the speaking stage
- The homework task lacked a model or example to guide students
- Overall lesson was well-planned and showed strong pedagogical knowledge but missed opportunities to push students beyond basic comprehension into analysis and to provide feedback on productive skills
