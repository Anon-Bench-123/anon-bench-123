# Lesson Observation Notes

**Teacher**: Ms R. (CELTA, DELTA, 12 years experience)
**Class**: B1 Adult General English, 14 students
**Institute**: Private language institute, Santiago, Chile
**Date**: [redacted]
**Lesson duration**: 50 minutes
**Topic**: Functional language -- making and responding to suggestions
**Observer**: Academic Coordinator

---

## Stage 1: Lead-in (0:00-0:08)

Teacher projected a short email exchange between two colleagues planning a team outing. Students read the email in pairs and discussed what the colleagues agreed to do. Good energy in the room. Teacher elicited answers from three different pairs. She used the email naturally to draw attention to the phrases "Why don't we..." and "How about..." which appeared in the text. Students seemed engaged and several volunteered answers without being nominated.

## Stage 2: Language presentation (0:08-0:22)

Teacher displayed a table of eight target phrases on the projector:
- "Why don't we...?"
- "How about + -ing...?"
- "What about + -ing...?"
- "Let's..."
- "I suggest + -ing..."
- "We could..."
- "Shall we...?"
- "That sounds good / great idea / I'm not sure about that / I'd rather..."

Teacher read through each phrase, gave one example sentence for each, and asked students to repeat chorally. She asked the class "Do you understand?" after each phrase. Most students nodded. She then asked students to copy the phrases into their notebooks. This stage took about 14 minutes.

No explicit attention was given to differences in formality or directness between phrases (e.g. "I suggest" vs "How about"). Teacher did not ask concept-checking questions or test whether students could distinguish when to use one phrase over another.

## Stage 3: Controlled practice (0:22-0:30)

Teacher distributed a gap-fill worksheet with 6 short dialogues. Students had to complete each gap with an appropriate suggestion phrase from the table. Students worked individually. After about 5 minutes, teacher asked for volunteers to read out answers. She confirmed correct answers but time was short so she went through the last two items quickly without checking whether students had different answers. Two students at the back appeared to still be working when the teacher moved on.

## Stage 4: Role-play -- freer practice (0:30-0:46)

Teacher gave each pair a role-play card with a scenario (e.g. planning a weekend trip, deciding on a restaurant for a birthday dinner, choosing a gift for a colleague). Students practised making and responding to suggestions in pairs, then switched partners and repeated with a new card. Energy in the room was good. Teacher circulated and listened.

Observer noted that most pairs relied heavily on "Why don't we..." and "Let's..." and did not experiment much with the other phrases. Three pairs used only these two phrases throughout both role-plays. Teacher did not intervene to encourage broader phrase use.

Teacher collected some errors on a notepad during the role-plays.

## Stage 5: Feedback and wrap-up (0:46-0:50)

Teacher wrote four error sentences on the board from the role-plays and asked the class to correct them together. Students responded well. Teacher then said "Great work today, keep practising these phrases" and the lesson ended.

No summary or review of the full range of phrases was conducted. No check was made of whether students could distinguish between the phrases or use a wider range. No homework or follow-up was set.

---

## General notes

- Classroom atmosphere was positive and relaxed throughout
- Teacher has confident, natural manner and good rapport with students
- Materials were well-prepared and professionally presented
- Timing was tight in the second half of the lesson; controlled practice felt rushed
- The lead-in was effective and contextualised the language well
- The language presentation was thorough but teacher-fronted with limited student involvement beyond choral repetition and copying
- Checking of learning was limited to "Do you understand?" with no concept-checking or discrimination tasks
- During freer practice, students defaulted to the phrases they already knew rather than experimenting with new ones
