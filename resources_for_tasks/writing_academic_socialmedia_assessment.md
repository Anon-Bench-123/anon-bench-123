# Academic Essay First Drafts - C1 Master's Students

## Context
**Course:** Academic Writing for Master's Students
**Level:** C1 (CEFR)
**Institution:** UK University
**Students:** 15 international master's students
**Assignment:** First draft of 1000-word argumentative essay
**Topic:** "To what extent has social media transformed political engagement among young people?"

---

## Essay Sample 1: Student from China

### Draft Text

Social media is very important thing in modern society. Many young people use it every day. This essay will discuss about social media and political engagement.

Firstly, social media makes young people more interested in politics. According to Smith (2020) says that 67% of young people get their news from social media. They can also share their opinions with their friends.

However, there are also disadvantages. Some people think that social media is not reliable. Anyone can post anything and it might be fake news. Johnson (2019) found that fake news spreads faster than real news. Also, social media can create echo chambers.

On the other hand, social media has created new ways for young people to participate in politics. The Arab Spring is a good example of how social media helped people fight for democracy. This shows that social media can be powerful tool for political change.

In conclusion, social media has had a big impact on political engagement among young people. It has both positive and negative effects.

## Essay Sample 2: Student from Brazil

### Draft Text

The rapid proliferation of social media platforms over the past two decades has fundamentally altered the landscape of political communication. This essay argues that while social media has transformed political engagement by democratizing access to information, it has simultaneously introduced significant challenges related to information quality and polarization.

Social media has transformed engagement through the democratization of political discourse. Young people can now follow politicians directly, effectively bypassing traditional media gatekeepers (Loader and Mercea, 2019). This disintermediation has profound implications, creating a more participatory form of politics.

Moreover, social media has proven effective for political mobilization. Hashtag activism - exemplified by #MeToo and #BlackLivesMatter - demonstrates how social media can amplify marginalized voices and coordinate collective action (Tufekci, 2017).

However, algorithmic curation creates "filter bubbles" that expose users primarily to information reinforcing existing beliefs (Pariser, 2011). This algorithmic polarization intensifies political tribalism rather than fostering deliberative democracy.

Furthermore, the rapid spread of misinformation poses serious threats. False news spreads faster than accurate information, primarily because such content tends to be more emotionally provocative (Vosoughi et al., 2018).

To conclude, social media has both democratized political engagement and created new challenges around information quality and polarization. The key is developing media literacy skills among young people to navigate this complex landscape effectively.
