# Writing Assessment: Legal Contract Analysis

**Student**: C1 professional, aged 28-35, Baku, Azerbaijan
**Task**: Memo analyzing employment contract for client
**Key Issues**: Precision in legal language, conditional complexity, hedging appropriateness

---

## Student's Memo (550 words)

MEMORANDUM

TO: Amir Hasanov, Managing Director
FROM: Legal Department
DATE: October 15, 2025
RE: Analysis of Employment Contract for Senior Software Engineer Position

Introduction

This memo provides analysis of the employment contract drafted for the Senior Software Engineer position. I reviewed the contract thoroughly and identified several areas that need attention before finalizing.

Non-Compete Clause (Section 7)

The non-compete clause is too restrictive. It prevents the employee from working for any technology company within 500 kilometers of Baku for three years after leaving our company. This is problematic for several reasons.

First, courts in Azerbaijan typically will not enforce non-compete agreements that are overly broad in geographic scope or duration. Three years is very long, especially in the fast-moving technology sector. Most comparable contracts in our industry limit non-compete periods to 6-12 months. If we try to enforce this clause, a court will probably strike it down as unreasonable.

Second, the 500-kilometer radius is extremely wide. It covers not only Baku but also most of central Azerbaijan. This would mean the employee couldn't work anywhere in the country for three years. This makes the contract unfair and could damage our reputation as employer if word spreads that we impose such harsh restrictions.

I recommend to reduce the non-compete period to 12 months maximum and limit the geographic scope to direct competitors in the Baku metropolitan area only. This would still protect our business interests while being more enforceable.

Intellectual Property Rights (Section 9)

Section 9 states that all intellectual property created by the employee "during employment" belongs to the company. However, this language is ambiguous. Does "during employment" mean:
- Only work created during working hours?
- Anything created while employed, including personal projects on weekends?
- Only work related to company projects?

This ambiguity could lead to disputes. If the employee develops personal software projects in their free time using their own equipment, they might argue these belong to them, not the company.

I suggest we clarify this section to specify that the company owns IP created: (1) during working hours, (2) using company resources, or (3) related to company business, current or reasonably anticipated. This formulation protects company interests while respecting employee's right to pursue personal projects.

Termination Provisions (Section 11)

The termination provisions strongly favor the employer. The company can terminate "for cause" based on "unsatisfactory performance" without defining what this means. This is too vague and could be challenged.

Furthermore, if the employee is terminated, they must return all company property within 24 hours or face financial penalties. This is unrealistic. If someone is terminated on Friday afternoon, they might not be able to return equipment until Monday. The 24-hour requirement is impractical and unnecessarily punitive.

I recommend defining "unsatisfactory performance" more specifically with examples, and extending the property return deadline to 48 business hours.

Compensation and Benefits (Section 5)

The compensation package is competitive and clearly stated. No issues here.

Conclusion and Recommendations

While the overall structure of the contract is good, the non-compete clause, IP language, and termination provisions need revision before we present this to the candidate. Making these changes will create a fairer contract that is more likely to be enforceable and will reflect better on our company culture.

Please let me know if you would like me to draft revised language for these sections.

---

**Strengths**: Clear structure, identifies real issues, appropriate professional tone for internal memo
**Areas for Improvement**:
- Precision: "typically will not enforce" - in legal writing, should be more precise about the standard ("are unlikely to enforce" or "have consistently refused to enforce")
- Conditional complexity: "If we try to enforce this clause, a court will probably strike it down" - too simple for legal analysis; should use more complex conditional structures to express degrees of likelihood
- Hedging: Inconsistent hedging - "could be challenged" (appropriate), "will probably strike it down" (hedge with "would likely"), "is too vague" (absolute - should hedge with "may be too vague" in legal analysis)
- Recommendations: "I recommend to reduce" (should be "I recommend reducing" - verb pattern error)
- Legal precision: "most comparable contracts" - vague; legal analysis should cite specific precedents, regulations, or industry standards
- Numbered points formatting: Uses numbered list within sentence without proper formatting
