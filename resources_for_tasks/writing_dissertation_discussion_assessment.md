## Student's Discussion (700 words)

CHAPTER 5: DISCUSSION

5.1 Introduction

This chapter discusses the findings presented in Chapter 4 in relation to the research questions and existing literature on mobile-assisted language learning (MALL) in sub-Saharan African contexts. The study investigated the effectiveness of SMS-based vocabulary instruction among secondary school students in rural Kenya, with particular attention to the role of limited internet connectivity and device ownership patterns.

5.2 Interpretation of Main Findings

The quantitative results demonstrate that students in the SMS intervention group achieved statistically significant vocabulary gains compared to the control group (p < 0.01), with a medium-to-large effect size (Cohen's d = 0.68). This finding aligns with previous research on mobile learning in resource-constrained environments, though the effect size observed in this study is somewhat larger than those reported in similar contexts by Nami (2020) and Baya'a and Daher (2021).

Several factors may account for the relative success of the SMS-based intervention. First, the high penetration of basic mobile phones in the study area (94% of households) meant that nearly all participants had consistent access to the intervention materials. This contrasts with smartphone-based interventions, which face significant equity issues in rural Kenya. Second, the asynchronous nature of SMS messages allowed students to engage with vocabulary items at their convenience, which may have reduced cognitive load and facilitated deeper processing. Third, the regular spacing of messages (daily) likely benefited from the spacing effect well-documented in cognitive psychology literature.

However, the qualitative data reveals a more complex picture. While focus group interviews confirmed that students found the SMS messages helpful and motivating, several participants reported challenges. The character limit of SMS messages (160 characters) sometimes resulted in oversimplified definitions that lacked nuance. Moreover, the inability to include visual aids or pronunciation guides limited the intervention's effectiveness for vocabulary items where form-meaning mapping is complex. These limitations suggest that SMS-based instruction, while valuable, cannot completely replace face-to-face vocabulary instruction or more media-rich digital interventions.

The socioeconomic analysis introduces another layer of complexity. Students from households with higher literacy levels showed greater gains from the SMS intervention than those from households with lower literacy levels (interaction effect: p = 0.03). This finding is concerning because it suggests that technology interventions, even relatively simple ones like SMS, may inadvertently exacerbate existing educational inequalities rather than ameliorating them. This contradicts the assumption, prevalent in much MALL literature, that mobile technologies are inherently democratizing forces in education.

5.3 Theoretical Implications

These findings have several implications for theory in mobile-assisted language learning. First, they provide empirical support for the accessibility dimension of Kim and Kwon's (2012) framework for mobile learning design, demonstrating that matching the technological sophistication of interventions to local infrastructure is crucial for effectiveness. The success of SMS-based instruction in this context, where app-based or web-based interventions would have failed due to connectivity issues, underscores the importance of context-sensitive pedagogical technology design.

Second, the differential effects observed across socioeconomic groups suggest that we need more nuanced theoretical models that account for intersectionality in mobile learning. The current frameworks in MALL research typically treat "access" as a binary variable (have device/no device), but this study demonstrates that meaningful access involves not only device ownership but also household literacy, digital literacy, and other socio-cultural factors. Future theoretical work should incorporate these variables more explicitly.

Third, the study contributes to our understanding of vocabulary acquisition in second language contexts by demonstrating that even minimal technological interventions can yield measurable learning gains when designed with careful attention to cognitive principles (spacing, retrieval practice). This suggests that the pedagogical design of mobile interventions may be more important than their technological sophistication – a finding with obvious implications for resource-constrained educational contexts.

5.4 Methodological Limitations

Several limitations of this study warrant discussion and suggest directions for future research. The relatively short intervention period (8 weeks) means that we cannot draw conclusions about long-term vocabulary retention or integration of learned vocabulary into productive language use. Additionally, the vocabulary test used, while validated, measured primarily receptive knowledge (recognition) rather than productive knowledge (recall), which limits our understanding of the intervention's full impact. The study also focused exclusively on vocabulary learning, leaving questions about the applicability of SMS-based instruction to other language skills (grammar, pronunciation, pragmatics) unanswered.

Furthermore, the study was conducted in a specific geographic and linguistic context (rural Kenya, English as a foreign language) and findings may not generalize straightforwardly to urban contexts, other African countries, or other target languages.
