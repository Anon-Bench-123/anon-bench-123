## Student's Abstract and Introduction (650 words)

**Title**: An Investigation into the Utilization of Translanguaging Practices in English Medium Instruction Contexts: A Case Study Examination of Bangladeshi Tertiary Education Institutions

**Abstract**

The implementation of English Medium Instruction (EMI) in higher education contexts in non-Anglophone countries has been experiencing rapid expansion in recent decades. This study represents an undertaking of an examination of translanguaging practices within three private universities in Dhaka, Bangladesh, where EMI policies have been adopted for the purpose of instruction delivery. Through the employment of classroom observations, teacher interviews, and student focus groups, this research makes an investigation into how multilingual practices are utilized by both instructors and students despite official monolingual policies. The findings reveal that there is a prevalence of translanguaging as a pedagogical resource for the facilitation of content comprehension, the building of rapport, and the management of classroom dynamics. However, the study also identifies the existence of tensions between institutional policies promoting English-only instruction and actual classroom practices that demonstrate linguistic flexibility. These results have implications for the understanding of the effectiveness of EMI policies in multilingual contexts and suggest the need for more explicit acknowledgment of translanguaging as a legitimate pedagogical tool in tertiary education settings.

**Keywords**: English Medium Instruction, translanguaging, multilingualism, Bangladesh, higher education

**1. INTRODUCTION**

1.1 Background to the Study

The adoption of English Medium Instruction (EMI) in higher education has undergone significant growth globally over the past two decades (Macaro, 2018). In contexts where English is not spoken as a first language, there has been an increasing trend of universities making the decision to offer programs taught entirely in English, with motivations including the attraction of international students, the improvement of graduates' employability, and the enhancement of institutional prestige (Dearden, 2015).

In Bangladesh, the proliferation of private universities offering EMI programs has been rapid since the early 2000s. Currently, there are over 100 private universities in Bangladesh, with the majority of them conducting instruction in English (University Grants Commission, 2022). This shift toward EMI represents a significant departure from the traditional Bengali-medium instruction that has been the norm in most public universities. The adoption of EMI has been driven by the perception that English language proficiency is essential for participation in the globalized economy and for access to international scholarship and research (Rahman, 2021).

However, the implementation of EMI policies in Bangladesh faces numerous challenges. Many students who are entering these universities have been educated in Bengali-medium schools and possess limited proficiency in English. Similarly, many faculty members, despite being highly qualified in their disciplinary areas, may not have received training in teaching content through English. These contextual factors create a situation where the strict enforcement of English-only policies may impede learning rather than facilitating it.

1.2 The Concept of Translanguaging

Translanguaging, a term coined by Williams (1994) and developed further by Garcia and Wei (2014), refers to the practice of multilingual speakers drawing upon their full linguistic repertoires in flexible and dynamic ways. Rather than conceptualizing languages as separate systems that should be kept distinct, translanguaging theory argues that multilinguals possess an integrated linguistic system and naturally move fluidly between what are traditionally conceived of as separate languages.

In educational contexts, translanguaging has been gaining recognition as a pedagogical approach that can support learning by allowing students to use all of their linguistic resources. Research has shown that translanguaging can facilitate comprehension of complex content, enable more sophisticated cognitive processing, and support students' identity development (Cenoz & Gorter, 2022).

Despite the growing body of research on translanguaging in primary and secondary education, there has been relatively limited research examining translanguaging practices in higher education EMI contexts, particularly in South Asian settings. This study makes an attempt to address this gap through an examination of how translanguaging functions in Bangladeshi university classrooms.
