## Student's Literature Review (600 words)

Literature Review: Code-Switching in Multilingual Educational Contexts

Code-switching, which is the practice of alternating between two or more languages within a single conversation or discourse, has been studied by many researchers over the past few decades. This literature review will examine the main theories and findings about code-switching in educational settings.

Early Research on Code-Switching

The study of code-switching began in the 1970s and 1980s. Gumperz (1982) was one of the first researchers who studied this phenomenon in detail. He found that code-switching is not random but follows specific patterns. According to Gumperz, speakers use code-switching for various functions like emphasizing a point, quoting someone, or showing group identity. Myers-Scotton (1993) also studied code-switching and developed the Matrix Language Frame model. This model explains how one language serves as the base language while elements from another language are inserted.

All researchers agree that code-switching is a natural part of bilingual communication. It is clear that code-switching shows linguistic competence rather than linguistic deficiency. Poplack (1980) proved this through her research on Spanish-English bilinguals in New York. She demonstrated that code-switching follows grammatical rules and constraints.

Code-Switching in Educational Contexts

When it comes to education, there are different views about code-switching. Some educators believe that code-switching in the classroom is bad and should be avoided. They argue that it can confuse students and prevent them from fully acquiring the target language. Garcia (2009) disagrees with this view. She says that code-switching is a valuable pedagogical tool that can help students understand difficult concepts.

Recent studies support the positive view of code-switching in education. Lin (2013) studied code-switching practices in Hong Kong classrooms and found that teachers use code-switching to explain complex ideas, manage classroom behavior, and build rapport with students. Similarly, Probyn (2015) studied South African classrooms and found that code-switching helps students understand scientific concepts when they switch between English and their home languages.

The research shows that code-switching can improve learning outcomes. Ferguson (2009) conducted a study in secondary schools and found that students who were allowed to use code-switching in discussions showed better understanding of the material compared to students in English-only classrooms. This is important because it challenges the traditional view that classroom should be monolingual.

Code-Switching and Identity

Another important aspect of code-switching in education is its connection to identity. Creese and Blackledge (2010) argue that code-switching allows students to maintain their cultural and linguistic identities while learning new languages. This is especially relevant in multilingual countries like the Philippines, where students speak multiple languages at home and are educated in English and Filipino.

Canagarajah (2011) takes this further by connecting code-switching to the concept of translanguaging. He suggests that multilingual students don't switch between separate language systems but rather draw from a single linguistic repertoire. This perspective has influenced recent educational policies in some countries.

Conclusion

The literature demonstrates that attitudes toward code-switching in education have changed significantly. While early research focused on describing the linguistic patterns of code-switching, recent studies examine its pedagogical implications. There is growing consensus that code-switching is a natural and valuable resource in multilingual classrooms. However, more research is needed to understand how teachers can effectively use code-switching as a pedagogical strategy without compromising target language development.

**Areas for Improvement**:
- Academic hedging: Too many absolute statements - "All researchers agree" (overgeneralization), "proves" (too strong - prefer "suggests" or "indicates"), "It is clear that" (lacks appropriate hedging)
- Citation integration: Overreliance on "X says/found that" structure - needs more variety ("According to X," "X's research indicates," "As demonstrated by X")
- Synthesis: Mostly describes sources sequentially rather than synthesizing themes across sources - should group studies by theme rather than chronologically listing each one
- Critical analysis: Lacks critical evaluation of sources - presents all findings as equally valid without questioning methodology or limitations
