## Excerpt 1: Argumentative Essay (submitted October, Week 3)

**Prompt**: Should universities abolish traditional exams in favour of continuous assessment?

The question of whether universities should replace traditional exams with continuous assessment is a topic that provokes strong opinions on both sides. In my opinion, I think that continuous assessment is the better option for several reasons which I will explain in this essay.

Firstly, traditional exams test only the ability of memorising information in a short time. Many students learn everything one or two weeks before the exam and then forget it after. This is not real learning. On the other hand, continuous assessment makes that students have to engage with the material during the whole semester.

Secondly, exams cause a lot of stress and anxiety for students. Some students who are actually very knowledgeable perform badly because they are nervous. With continuous assessment, the pressure is spreaded more evenly across the semester.

However, some people argue that exams are necessary because they ensure all students are tested under the same conditions. This is a valid point, but I would argue that standardisation is not more important as fairness.

In conclusion, while both systems have their merits, continuous assessment is the more fair and effective approach to evaluating student learning in universities.

## Excerpt 2: Literature Review Section (submitted November, Week 8)

**Prompt**: Write a literature review section on the effectiveness of technology-enhanced language learning.

Research into technology-enhanced language learning (TELL) has expanded considerably over the past decade. This review examines key findings relating to the impact of TELL on language acquisition outcomes and learner engagement.

Several meta-analyses have demonstrated a modest but statistically significant positive effect of technology on language learning outcomes. However, as Stockwell (2012) notes, the mere presence of technology does not guarantee improved results; rather, it is the pedagogical design underpinning its use that determines effectiveness. This is supported by Golonka et al. (2014), whose systematic review found that while certain tools showed promise, the evidence base remained inconclusive for many technologies due to methodological weaknesses.

A recurring theme is the potential of technology to enhance learner autonomy. Reinders and Wattana (2015) reported that game-based learning environments increased willingness to communicate, though they cautioned that gains were context-dependent. Similarly, Lai and Gu (2011) found that successful self-directed technology use was strongly mediated by learners prior digital literacy and metacognitive skills, suggesting that access to tools alone is not sufficient.

One notable gap is the scarcity of longitudinal studies examining sustained technology use over periods longer than a single academic term. Most intervention studies span 8-16 weeks, which may be insufficient to capture longer-term effects on proficiency development.

In summary, technology can serve as a valuable complement to language instruction when integrated thoughtfully, but it is not a replacement for sound pedagogical principles.

## Excerpt 3: Reflective Essay (submitted January, Week 15)

**Prompt**: Reflect on your development as an academic writer this semester.

When I look back at the beginning of this semester, I can see that my academic writing has developed in several important ways, though there are still areas where I need to continue working.

At the start of the course, my biggest weakness was that I wrote in a style that was too personal and informal for academic contexts. I used phrases like "I think" and "in my opinion" very frequently, and my arguments relied more on what I felt than on evidence.

Over the semester, I have become much more comfortable with the conventions of academic writing. I have learnt to use hedging language rather than making absolute claims. I have also improved my ability to synthesise sources rather than simply summarising them one after another. My literature review represents what I consider my biggest leap forward -- I managed to organise it thematically rather than source-by-source.

One area where I have struggled is with articles. In German, we have articles but they work differently, and I still sometimes omit the definite article where English requires it. My tutor has pointed this out repeatedly but it remains a persistent difficulty.

Another ongoing challenge is collocations. I sometimes translate directly from German, producing phrases that are understandable but not natural -- for example, I initially wrote "take a decision" because in German we say "eine Entscheidung treffen" which is closer to "take" than "make."

Overall, I am satisfied with my progress but I know there is still considerable work to do before my writing reaches the level I need for my master's thesis.
